The Power of Regulation

From NYC data centers to the Montreal Protocol — and back to building policy

Global warming is a pressing crisis shaped by two forces: the built environment and policy.

This project uses CO₂‑equivalent (CO₂e) as a shared metric to connect atmospheric change, building performance, and regulation across scales.

We begin with New York City’s embedded data centers, step back to the Montreal Protocol as a global success story, and return to LEED to ask how building‑scale regulation can — and cannot — manage rapidly growing digital infrastructure.

PART 1

Data Centers in New York City

NYC’s large buildings that report hosting data centers emitted approximately 3.6 million metric tons of CO₂e in 2023—roughly equal to the annual electricity-related emissions of ~480,000 U.S. homes (EPA equivalencies, using ~7.5 metric tons CO₂ per home per year). That’s more households than all of San Francisco.

But unlike the hyperscale campuses being built in rural Virginia, Ohio, or Iowa, New York’s data center boom is happening vertically—tucked inside ordinary office towers. These embedded facilities often occupy only a fraction of a building’s floor area, yet they exert an outsized impact on energy demand.

From 2023 to 2024, the city added 220,057.8 sq ft of reported data center space, even as the number of buildings reporting data centers decreased. In other words, fewer buildings now host far larger and more energy-intensive data centers.

Using CO₂‑equivalent (CO₂e) as a shared metric, we trace how this footprint concentrates into fewer, more energy‑intensive buildings—and why data centers behave like a distinct, continuous‑load use case that existing building‑wide frameworks do not explicitly isolate.

NYC’s Data Center Growth Is Likely Underestimated

This increase is likely an underestimate. More than a hundred office buildings that reported data center floor area in 2023 failed to submit benchmarking data in 2024— largely due to sales, renovations, or partial building closures. Many of these buildings historically contained substantial data center loads. As they re-enter the dataset over the next reporting cycles, NYC’s true data center footprint is likely to appear even larger.

PART 2

Data Centers Accelerating

The NYC case sits inside a broader shift: data centers are already a major electricity load in the United States, and demand is expected to rise as AI workloads expand and more services move into the cloud.

This may not seem like much at first glance, but growth is shaped by multiple coupled factors: cooling systems can drive substantial water use, and the emissions impact depends on the grid mix. Where electricity remains fossil-heavy, data center expansion translates directly into higher CO₂e.

The next charts step outward—from a comparative snapshot to a U.S. projection—to show why rapid growth without sector-specific standards becomes a policy problem that ultimately lands in cities.

Multiple studies point to a steep near-term rise in U.S. data center electricity demand. According to U.S. Congressional reporting, data centers used roughly 176 TWh in 2023 (excluding cryptocurrency)—about 4.4% of total U.S. electricity.

Many projections expect demand to double or triple within a few years, driven by AI workloads and cloud expansion. In practice, this curve can rise faster than grids and clean-energy supply can adapt.

Crucially, binding, operations-specific energy standards for private-sector data centers remain limited. Without sector-specific disclosure and performance requirements, growth can outpace what existing policies are designed to measure.

These national trajectories do not remain abstract. As demand accelerates, it ultimately materializes inside cities—within buildings, local grids, and emissions caps like those in New York City.

Global Distribution of Data Centers

At the global scale, data centers are unevenly distributed. A small number of countries host a large share of the world’s facilities, concentrating energy use, water consumption, and CO₂e emissions in particular regions. Unlike the case of CFCs in the 1980s, this growth has not yet been matched by a binding international agreement.

PART 3–1

Ozone Recovery and the Montreal Protocol

Next, we step back to an earlier atmospheric crisis to see what effective regulation can achieve. In the 1970s and 80s, scientists warned that chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) were thinning the ozone layer— an invisible but globally shared system.

The Montreal Protocol, agreed in 1987 and entering into force in 1989, became a turning point: a proof that invisible atmospheric damage could be reversed through binding international regulation.

The Montreal Protocol: A Timeline of Global Action

The Montreal Protocol is often described as one of humanity’s most successful environmental treaties. It followed decades of scientific warnings about CFCs and their impact on the ozone layer. Through coordinated global action, nations agreed to phase out ozone-depleting substances — and satellite data now shows a gradual healing of the ozone layer.

Later amendments expanded the treaty’s scope to additional chemicals and strengthened enforcement mechanisms. Crucially, the Protocol translated an invisible atmospheric problem into measurable targets, shared responsibility, and binding timelines—creating a template for governing other high-impact systems.

1974

Mario Molina and F. Sherwood Rowland discover that CFCs destroy ozone molecules, marking the beginning of modern atmospheric chemistry awareness.

1985

Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer is adopted, establishing a scientific and legal framework for international cooperation.

1987

The Montreal Protocol is signed by 46 countries, committing to phase out ozone-depleting substances like CFCs and halons.

1989

The Montreal Protocol enters into force, beginning the legally binding global phaseout of key ozone-depleting chemicals.

1990

London Amendments strengthen phaseout deadlines and provide funding for developing nations to transition away from harmful chemicals.

2016

Kigali Amendment expands the Protocol to include HFCs, explicitly linking ozone recovery with climate mitigation.

2024

NASA and WMO confirm record ozone recovery over Antarctica, projecting near-complete restoration of pre-1980 ozone levels by mid-century.

PART 3–2

LEED and Building-Scale Regulation

The Montreal Protocol worked by turning atmospheric science into enforceable rules. At the city scale, LEED translates a similar logic by converting building performance—energy, water, and emissions— into measurable criteria that influence design, operations, and investment.

LEED demonstrates how regulation can shape building performance—but it also reveals where building-scale metrics begin to break down for energy-intensive uses like data centers.

So how should NYC regulate embedded data centers?

NYC already regulates building performance, but data centers behave like a distinct use: continuous loads, intensive cooling, and rapid expansion inside mixed-use towers. The next step is to complement building-wide policy with data center–specific requirements.

Policy directions (concept)

  • Disclosure: require standardized reporting of data center floor area and IT load within buildings.
  • Performance: set targets for efficiency (e.g., EUI/IT load) and cooling/water performance where applicable.
  • Grid alignment: link expansion to clean electricity procurement and demand-response participation.
  • Lifecycle accountability: track embodied impacts and equipment turnover for rapidly changing infrastructure.

LEED, in one minute

LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a point-based green building rating system developed by the U.S. Green Building Council (USGBC). Projects earn points across categories like energy, water, and indoor environmental quality.

Why it matters for data centers

Data centers are a unique building type: few occupants, but continuous, high-intensity loads. Cooling demand is often the dominant driver, and water use can be significant when water-based cooling is used. LEED rewards strategies like efficient HVAC, on-site renewables, and water-saving systems.

Where it fits in NYC policy

LL84 makes performance visible (disclosure). LL97 converts visibility into obligations (caps). LEED shapes design culture (what “good” looks like). Together, they form a feedback loop between design standards and operational data.

Key takeaway

LEED shows that policy can shape performance—but it also exposes a limitation. Embedded data centers can overwhelm building-level metrics even when they comply formally, raising the question of whether sector-specific rules are needed.

Data Centers Need Regulation

The Montreal Protocol shows that strong, science‑based regulation can reverse damage in invisible systems.

LEED demonstrates that building‑scale policy can shape performance — but it also reveals limits when energy‑intensive uses are embedded inside larger buildings.

Data centers are expanding rapidly while remaining largely invisible inside buildings and datasets. New York’s case shows why they need data center–specific disclosure and performance standards— so digital infrastructure does not become a long‑lived layer of urban emissions inside an already regulated city.
