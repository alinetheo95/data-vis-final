The Invisible Layers

Environmental Impact of the Digital Economy

An examination of data center carbon emissions in the context of proven regulatory frameworks. Through comparative analysis of the Montreal Protocol’s success in reversing ozone depletion and LEED’s transformation of building performance standards, this research demonstrates how measurement, transparency, and policy intervention can address the rapidly growing environmental burden of digital infrastructure.

PART 1

The Built Environment

The built environment–our buildings, infrastructure, and energy systems–accounts for 38% of U.S. carbon dioxide emissions and consumes 72% of our electricity. Despite this enormous footprint, much of this infrastructure operates without comprehensive environmental oversight. As architects and designers, we have both an opportunity and a responsibility to address this crisis at its source: through thoughtful design, as well as advocacy for effective policy.

The Standardization of Greenhouse Gas Emissions

CO2e, or carbon dioxide equivalent, is a standardized metric used to measure and compare the climate impact of various greenhouse gases. This includes, but is not limited to, key greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases (CFCs, HFCs, HCFCs). Each greenhouse gas is assigned a global warming potential value that represents its warming impact over a specific period relative to carbon dioxide. The equation below allows for a standardization providing a common unit of measurement so businesses and countries can report total climate change impact as a single, comparable number.

CO2e = (Mass of GHG) x (GWP of the gas)

The standardization of all greenhouse gases can help governments track and manage emissions and report progression towards shared climate goals, and allow for introduction of policy.

The Rise of Data Centers and CO2

Data centers are essential infrastructure in the digital economy, yet their rapidly growing carbon footprint is largely exempt from environmental regulation. The construction of data centers has increased tremendously with the introduction of LLM’s in recent years. Data centers require copious amounts of energy to operate, accounting for 1.8% of electricity use in the United States. This may not seem like much, but studies have shown that with the rapid growth developments of AI, this percentage is predicted to increase exponentially over the next decade. With no regulations currently in place for data centers, the energy usage can quickly become damaging to the environment and will continue to deplete our resources.

Data source: Geocoded NYC data centers derived from NYC Building Energy and Water Data Disclosure for Local Law 84 (2020–2024).

The sources of energy for these data centers also matter. According to a recent study, approximately 56% of the electricity consumed by data centers in the United States came from fossil fuel-burning power plants, and 16% was from coal power plants. This high dependence on coal is a significant contributor to the carbon footprint of the data center industry.

Distance compounds the problem. Data centers require massive amounts of water for cooling, and this water is often sourced from far away, requiring additional energy for pumping and transport. About one-fifth of data center servers’ direct water footprint comes from moderately to highly water-stressed watersheds, meaning facilities are drawing resources from regions that can least afford to spare them. When non-renewable energy sources, long distance resource transmission, and geographic inefficiency are added together, researchers estimate data centers contribute 0.5% of total carbon emissions.

The Transparency Gap

0.5% of carbon emissions may not seem like much, but currently these data centers are not required to report energy usage as public information. In our research, we found that publicly available data for data centers is limited or behind a paywall, making it difficult to determine exactly how damaging data center operations currently are and will become. The lack of mandatory reporting makes it impossible for researchers, policy makers, and the public to understand the true scale of the problem.

Location Matters: The Geography of Carbon

Not all data centers carry the same carbon footprint. A facility in New York City drawing from the region’s relatively diversified grid, including nuclear, hydroelectric, and natural gas, produces significantly less CO2 than an identical facility in the Midwest powered primarily by coal. Without policy frameworks that account for grid carbon intensity, the market has no mechanism to discourage construction in high-emission regions.

While specific energy data for individual data centers in the Midwest was not publicly available at the time of this study, we can infer that coal-dependent areas carry substantially higher carbon footprints than their New York City counterparts. Additionally, Midwestern data centers tend to be more geographically dispersed and must service population centers located farther away, resulting in greater transmission losses and network energy consumption, ultimately increasing their total carbon impact even greater. PART 2

Data Centers Accelerating

The NYC case sits inside a broader shift: data centers are already a major electricity load in the United States, and demand is expected to rise as AI workloads expand and more services move into the cloud.

This may not seem like much at first glance, but growth is shaped by multiple coupled factors: cooling systems can drive substantial water use, and the emissions impact depends on the grid mix. Where electricity remains fossil-heavy, data center expansion translates directly into higher CO₂e.

Multiple studies point to a steep near-term rise in U.S. data center electricity demand. According to U.S. Congressional reporting, data centers used roughly 176 TWh in 2023 (excluding cryptocurrency)—about 4.4% of total U.S. electricity.

Many projections expect demand to double or triple within a few years, driven by AI workloads and cloud expansion. In practice, this curve can rise faster than grids and clean-energy supply can adapt.

Crucially, binding, operations-specific energy standards for private-sector data centers remain limited. Without sector-specific disclosure and performance requirements, growth can outpace what existing policies are designed to measure.

These national trajectories do not remain abstract. As demand accelerates, it ultimately materializes inside cities—within buildings, local grids, and emissions caps like those in New York City.

Why Policy Works: From Measurement to Action

Data centers operate without mandatory emissions reporting, efficiency standards, or accountability for their growing share of national energy demand. Meanwhile, the environmental challenges we have alleviated share a common pattern: comprehensive policy frameworks built on transparent data, enforceable standards, and international cooperation.

As we face the exponential growth of AI-driven infrastructure, we don’t need to reinvent the policy playbook. We need to apply the lessons we’ve already learned to the technologies reshaping our energy future.

PART 3–1

Ozone Recovery and the Montreal Protocol

The Montreal Protocol, signed in 1987, is often described as one of the most successful environmental treaties. It was put into place following decades of scientific warnings about chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), chemicals used in refrigerants and aerosols that were found to destroy the ozone layer. Through coordinated global action, nations agreed to phase out these substances, and satellite data now shows a gradual healing of the ozone layer - clear evidence that science, regulation, and collection action can reverse planetary damage.

The Montreal Protocol has continued to evolve as science and technology have advanced. Amendments such as those adopted in London and Kigali expanded its scope to include additional substances like hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs) and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). This living framework shows how international regulation can translate data into environmental recovery - an example for today’s challenge of governing carbon emissions from energy-intensive infrastructure.

1974

Mario Molina and F. Sherwood Rowland discover that CFCs destroy ozone molecules, marking the beginning of modern atmospheric chemistry awareness.

1985

Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer is adopted, establishing a scientific and legal framework for international cooperation.

1987

The Montreal Protocol is signed by 46 countries, committing to phase out ozone-depleting substances like CFCs and halons.

1989

The Montreal Protocol enters into force, beginning the legally binding global phaseout of key ozone-depleting chemicals.

1990

London Amendments strengthen phaseout deadlines and provide funding for developing nations to transition away from harmful chemicals.

2016

Kigali Amendment expands the Protocol to include HFCs, explicitly linking ozone recovery with climate mitigation.

2024

NASA and WMO confirm record ozone recovery over Antarctica, projecting near-complete restoration of pre-1980 ozone levels by mid-century.

Global Ozone Recovery After the Montreal Protocol

The Montreal Protocol (1987) is widely recognized as one of the most effective environmental agreements. By phasing out ozone-depleting substances such as CFCs and HFC’s, it set in motion a measurable recovery of the ozone layer within just a few decades.

The Montreal Protocol shows that collective regulation works: over 190 nations agreed to phase out ozone-depleting chemicals, and the results are visible in the atmosphere today. In contrast, carbon emissions from digital infrastructure continue to rise without consistent global or national policy frameworks. Our visualizations compare how the presence of strong policy versus its absence shapes the trajectory of atmospheric change and environmental recovery.

PART 3–2

LEED Certification: Translating Building Performance into Market Action

The Montreal Protocol worked by turning atmospheric science into enforceable rules. At the city scale, LEED translates a similar logic by converting building performance—energy, water, and emissions— into measurable criteria that influence design, operations, and investment.

The U.S. Green Building Council’s LEED certification system, launched in 2000, created a voluntary framework that transformed industry practice through transparency and standardization. By requiring buildings to measure and report energy consumption, water use, and material impacts, LEED made environmental performance visible and comparable.

LEED demonstrates how regulation can shape building performance—but it also reveals where building-scale metrics begin to break down for energy-intensive uses like data centers.

So how should NYC regulate embedded data centers?

NYC already regulates building performance, but data centers behave like a distinct use: continuous loads, intensive cooling, and rapid expansion inside mixed-use towers. The next step is to complement building-wide policy with data center–specific requirements.

Policy directions (concept)

  • Disclosure: require standardized reporting of data center floor area and IT load within buildings.
  • Performance: set targets for efficiency (e.g., EUI/IT load) and cooling/water performance where applicable.
  • Grid alignment: link expansion to clean electricity procurement and demand-response participation.
  • Lifecycle accountability: track embodied impacts and equipment turnover for rapidly changing infrastructure.

Why it matters for data centers

Data centers are a unique building type: few occupants, but continuous, high-intensity loads. Cooling demand is often the dominant driver, and water use can be significant when water-based cooling is used. LEED rewards strategies like efficient HVAC, on-site renewables, and water-saving systems.

Where it fits in NYC policy

LL84 makes performance visible (disclosure). LL97 converts visibility into obligations (caps). LEED shapes design culture (what “good” looks like). Together, they form a feedback loop between design standards and operational data.

Key takeaway

LEED shows that policy can shape performance—but it also exposes a limitation. Embedded data centers can overwhelm building-level metrics even when they comply formally, raising the question of whether sector-specific rules are needed.

Data Centers Need Regulation

History has shown us that voluntary measures alone cannot address environmental challenges at scale. The success of the Montreal Protocol in healing the ozone layer and LEED certification in transforming building practices demonstrates that policy frameworks can drive measurable change.

Policy Works - When it Exists

The Montreal Protocol engaged 197 nations to phase out ozone-depleting substances, proving the international cooperation can reverse planetary damage. LEED demonstrated that transparency and certification can transform entire industries without waiting for government mandates. Both frameworks share a foundation: measurement enables accountability, and accountability drives change.

As we enter a new era of data-driven and AI-driven growth, the same urgency and cooperation are needed to govern the emissions of the digital age. If coordinated policy has proven to work in multiple scenarios, it can again – but only if it is extended to the infrastructures that are now driving carbon emissions.
